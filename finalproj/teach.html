The Church's Disabilities website.

Part 1: Good Teaching is Good Teaching

Teaching using multiple modalities will help all types of learners to learn. For example, if a young child has difficulty sitting at a desk, then draw a number chart on the ground and have the child hop to do their math homework. Another example is the manipulatives used in classrooms at a desk. Think about involving the different senses of the learners when we try teach.

Part 2: Attention Getter

Some learners are more inclined to look when the instructor uses a pointer instead of a hand or finger. Some individuals with autism tend to be more focused on objects. Merely speaking is the least effective method to teach individuals who are more impacted by learning differences.

Part 3: State Objective

When the instructor asks the learner a question to try to gauge understanding, the instructor should switch the order of the choices. For example, ask, or better still, present or write the options. First present maybe the correct answer then the detractor. Afterwards switch and present the detractor then the correct answer. Some individuals with learning differences may reply with either the first or last thing they heard; this is the reason for switching the order of the options.

Part 4: Attention Span

Attention span is a curious thing. It is definitely important to provide frequent breaks. However, adapting the learner's environment can be helpful to sustain attention. For example, give the learner the option of sitting on a gym ball instead of a chair, or at the very least, offer a wiggle cushion. Another affordable option is to provide a pedal cycle under the desk so that the learner could have movement without distracting the others around. A more costly but effective alternative is to do reading and type notes at a walking treadmill desk.

Part 5: Using Visual Aids

Technology can be a lifeline to help those with moderate-severe developmental disabilities to learn complicated daily tasks. For example, the process of taking a shower involves a few tasks which involves many steps each. We could break down a task into the very small steps involved and teach the small steps. It is common that some individuals with moderate-severe developmental disabilities may have difficulty sequencing the steps to perform a task. This is where the blessing of technology helps to teach. We could use a tablet and upload the individual steps of a task as slides, then in time, even those with moderate-severe learning differences could learn to do complicated daily tasks for themselves.

Part 6: Wait Time

Yes, do give more time for individuals with learning differences to process before answering. In addition, consider options for those with some expressive language challenges to be able to answer.

Part 7: Active Participation

Having the learners engage one another in the learning process could be helpful for learners with mild learning differences. However, this might be difficult for learners with moderate-severe developmental disabilities.

Part 8: Music and Drama

This video is self-explanatory. Movement is also helpful in the learning process. For example, have the student go bring the correct answer to win tokens.

Part 9: Using a Schedule to Help Reduce Anxiety

This is important; reducing anxiety could help reduce behavioral issues. If the learner does not read yet, then a picture schedule could be use. A visual timer, like the Time Timer, which shows how much time is remaining for the current stage, has been helpful.

Part 10: Positive Behavior Strategies

In addition to what this video states, it is more effective communication when we speak in the affirmative. For example, instead of saying, "don't run," we should say, "please walk." When we say, "don't run," we have not provided clear instruction on what behavior we expect instead. Another example is, instead of saying, "don't sit on the tables," we should say, "please sit on the chair." By speaking in the affirmative, we provide clear instruction about what is expected.
