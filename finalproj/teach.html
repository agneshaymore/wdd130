Part 1: Good Teaching is Good Teaching

Teaching using multiple modalities will help all types of learners to learn. For example, if a young child has difficulty doing math homework sitting at a desk, then draw a number chart on the ground and have the child hop to do their math homework. A more common example we see in classrooms at a desk is to use manipulatives to help the students visualize. Think about involving the different senses of the learners when we try teach.

Part 2: Attention Getter

Some learners who are more object focused are more inclined to look when the instructor uses a pointer and visuals or objects. Merely speaking is the least effective method to teach individuals who are more impacted by learning differences.

Part 3: State Objective

When the instructor asks the learner a question to try to gauge understanding, the instructor should switch the order of the choices. For example, ask, present or write the options. First, present maybe the correct answer then the detractor. Afterwards, switch and present the detractor then the correct answer. Some individuals with learning differences may reply with either the first or last thing they heard; this is the reason for switching the order of the options.

Part 4: Attention Span

Attention span is a curious thing. Some students who have very short attention spans seem capable of fixating on something. It is definitely important to provide frequent breaks. However, adapting the learner's environment can be helpful to sustain attention.

For example, give the learner the option of sitting on a gym ball instead of a chair, or at the very least, offer a wiggle cushion. Another affordable option is to provide a pedal cycle under the desk so that the learner could have movement without distracting the others around. A more costly but effective alternative is to do reading and type notes at a walking treadmill desk. All these options provide the learner with movement on the spot, and thus may help sustain attention without distracting other learners in class.

The common figit objects we see used in classrooms are finger figit toys, textured balls, chewable pencil toppers or necklaces, and weighted vests. Even a simple thing like having the correct pencil grips could make it more comfortable for a learner to continue working.

Part 5: Using Visual Aids

Technology can be a lifeline to help those with developmental differences to learn complicated daily tasks. For example, the process of taking a shower involves a few tasks which are each comprised of many steps. We could break down a task into the small steps involved and teach each small step.

It is common for some individuals with developmental differences to have difficulty sequencing the steps involved to perform a task. This is where the blessing of technology helps us to teach. For example, we could use a tablet and upload the individual steps of a task as slides, then in time, even these learners with developmental differences could learn to do complicated daily tasks for themselves.

Another example is to use software to help learners organize themselves. These software not only provides a checklist, but also allows the caregiver or teacher to cluster tasks in a block if it does not matter which task is done first.

Part 6: Wait Time

Yes, do give more time for individuals with learning differences to process before answering. In addition, consider options for those with some expressive language challenges to be able to answer.

Another dimension to wait time is to teach a task in incrementally small steps to reduce anxiety. Also see Part 9 below. For example, learning to go to the doctor's or dentist's office could involve weeks of work. In week 1, we would drive to the place. In week 2, we would also get out of the car. In week 3, we would also walk across the parking lot. In week 4, we would also walk to the building entrance. In week 5, we would also walk inside the building to the lobby. In week 6, we would go upstairs. In week 7, we would go the the doctor's office lobby. In week 8, the doctor would say hello. In week 9, we may go inside the doctor's office. In week 10, the individual with developmental differences may let the doctor perform a cursory physical examination but if and only if the caregiver has practiced with the learner all these weeks at home what the doctor might do to examine the patient.

Helping the individual with developmental differences overcome sensory integration disorder issues also requires similar wait time to teach. For example, learning to tolerate a nail clipper involves months of desensitizing. The frequency used in this case was at least every other day. In week 1, place the nail clipper beside the learner. In week 2, the learner would touch the nail clipper. In week 3, the learner would hold the nail clipper and touch his/her own toes. In week 4, the learner might tolerate the caregiver touching his/her toes with the nail clipper. With some luck, in week 5, the learner might tolerate the caregiver clipping part of a toenail with a single clip. There is no magic formula, it just takes time.

Community safety awareness is more urgent to learn, but this still takes time to learn. It is safer to practice in a quiet location without too many cars driving by. For example, the learner with developmental differences has to learn to stand by the car when getting out instead of running around the parking lot unaware. Similarly, the learner has to learn to stop at the street corner and wait, but first the learner has to recognize what a street corner is. This involves visiting different street corners.

Note that in some cases, the learner's lack of generalization skills means that even though the learner may perform a task in a particular setting, the learner may have difficulty adapting his/her learned skill to a different setting.

Note that the learning process cannot be rushed because the learner's anxiety level the next time we try to teach would become more difficult to overcome.

Part 7: Active Participation

Having the learners engage one another in the learning process could be helpful for learners with mild learning differences. However, this might be difficult for learners with moderate-severe developmental disabilities.

Part 9: Using a Schedule to Help Reduce Anxiety

This is important; reducing anxiety could help reduce behavioral issues. If the learner does not read yet, then a picture schedule could be use. A visual timer, like the Time Timer, which shows how much time is remaining for the current stage, has been helpful.

Part 10: Positive Behavior Strategies

In addition to what this video states, it is more effective communication when we speak in the affirmative. For example, instead of saying, "don't run," we should say, "please walk." When we say, "don't run," we have not provided clear instruction on what behavior we expect instead. Another example is, instead of saying, "don't sit on the tables," we should say, "please sit on the chair." By speaking in the affirmative, we provide clear instruction about what is expected.
